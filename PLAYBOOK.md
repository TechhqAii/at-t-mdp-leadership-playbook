# AT&T MDP Executive Positioning Playbook

## Director’s decision

Do **not** position yourself as “the salesperson who also codes.”

Position yourself as:

> **A proven business operator who sees frontline friction, turns it into practical action, and is now learning to multiply results through people.**

That framing unifies all three parts of your record:

1. **Operator:** five years of consistently exceeding goals and multiple awards.
2. **Builder:** Gen AI Quote Quick won the 2024 AT&T Innovation Summit.
3. **Multiplier:** MDP is the bridge from individual excellence to broader team and organizational impact.

## What senior leaders should remember

After a good interaction, the internal sentence you want them to say is:

> “That is a strong seller with business judgment, practical innovation instincts, and leadership runway.”

Not:

- “He really wanted me to know every award.”
- “He has an AI side project.”
- “He was networking hard for a promotion.”

## Recommended 30-second introduction

> Hi, I’m **[name]**, an Account Executive in California. Over five years at AT&T, I’ve built a track record of consistently exceeding my goals. What differentiates me is that I don’t just work around friction—I look for ways to remove it. In 2024, that led me to build Gen AI Quote Quick, which won the AT&T Innovation Summit. I’m at MDP to strengthen how I turn frontline insight and disciplined execution into broader impact for customers and teams.

Use the exact role and market wording that feels natural internally. Do not add award names or metrics unless they genuinely improve the conversation.

## The Gen AI Quote Quick story

Use this order:

1. **Problem:** the exact sales or customer friction you observed.
2. **Action:** what the project changed in the workflow.
3. **Evidence:** the honest measured result, adoption, validation, or lesson.
4. **Leadership lesson:** how you involved users, earned adoption, handled risk, or connected the build to business value.

Fill these in before Sunday:

- Problem: **[one plain-English sentence]**
- Action: **[one plain-English sentence]**
- Evidence: **[one honest sentence; do not invent a number]**
- Leadership lesson: **[one sentence about people, adoption, judgment, or scale]**

The award is proof. It is not the entire story.

## The 5–3–1 event mission

- **5** substantive conversations
- **3** specific follow-ups you can act on
- **1** relationship worth intentionally continuing

A substantive conversation gives you at least one of these:

- a clearer view of the business;
- a leadership behavior to practice;
- a customer or seller problem worth understanding;
- a commitment to take a concrete action.

## Conversation sequence

### 1. Enter

Use the room before chasing hierarchy. Meet:

- one peer;
- one leader;
- one person outside your normal lane.

This gives you context and lowers the temptation to perform when you meet a senior executive.

### 2. Open

Choose one:

- “What part of the program are you most interested in?”
- “What is changing fastest in your part of the business?”
- “What is one field signal you wish more people upstream could see?”

### 3. Contribute

Use one short, relevant point. Do not force Gen AI Quote Quick into every conversation.

### 4. Exit

Choose one:

- “I don’t want to monopolize you—thank you. I’m taking that point with me.”
- “That was useful. I’m going to let you keep circulating.”
- “I’d like to follow up with the action I take from this, if that’s okay.”

## Hard questions and answer shapes

### “What do you want next?”

Do not answer only with a title.

Use:

1. the kind of problems or scope you want;
2. who benefits;
3. the capabilities you are preparing to earn;
4. one question back.

Example shape:

> I want broader responsibility where I can combine customer judgment, execution, and practical innovation to help a team perform—not just increase my own output. I know that requires a different set of muscles, especially coaching, clarity, and accountability. From your perspective, which capability most separates a high performer from a trusted first-line leader?

### “A strong seller is not automatically a strong manager.”

Agree. Do not become defensive.

> I agree. Personal output and creating the conditions for other people to win are different. My results show discipline and customer judgment; they do not automatically prove management readiness. What I’m building now is the ability to create clarity, coach instead of rescue, and help others own outcomes. That is one reason I value MDP.

### “What is a leadership habit you need to change?”

Use a real habit. One credible option—only if true—is moving from personally rescuing every outcome to creating clarity and ownership for others.

## Follow-up rule

A strong follow-up is not “great meeting you.” Use:

> Thank you for the point about **[specific idea]**. I said I would apply it, so my first action is **[specific action]**. I appreciated the clarity.

If a follow-up asks for time, keep the request small and specific.

## Final operating reminder

- Phone away.
- Hands visible.
- Pace ten percent slower.
- Ask one good question.
- Listen long enough to earn the bridge.
- End before the executive needs to end it for you.
- Selection means you belong. Curiosity and judgment will determine what people remember.
